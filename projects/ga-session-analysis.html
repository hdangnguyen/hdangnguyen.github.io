Case Study 1: E-Commerce Conversion Funnel Analysis

The Business Challenge

The product team knew traffic was healthy — but revenue wasn't growing proportionally. The core question was: where exactly are users dropping off in the buying journey? Without mapping the full funnel from product view to cart to purchase, there was no clear signal on whether the bottleneck was product discovery, cart friction, or checkout hesitation.

My Approach

1
Funnel Mapping via Action Types: Leveraged eCommerceAction.action_type codes to classify each hit — '2' = Product View, '3' = Add to Cart, '6' = Purchase.
2
Revenue Guard: Applied a productRevenue IS NOT NULL filter on purchase events to ensure only genuine completed transactions were counted, excluding abandoned checkouts.
3
Rate Calculation: Computed Add-to-Cart Rate (num_addtocart / num_product_view) and Purchase Rate (num_purchase / num_product_view) as percentages to make the funnel loss comparable across months.

Solution

The query uses a two-step CTE to first categorize hit events, then aggregate the funnel counts and calculate conversion rates per month:

SQL Query BigQuery (GA Sessions 2017) 
WITH
  data_overview AS(
  SELECT
    FORMAT_DATE('%Y%m',PARSE_DATE('%Y%m%d',date)) month,
    eCommerceAction.action_type action_type,
    totals.transactions,
    product.productRevenue
  FROM `bigquery-public-data.google_analytics_sample.ga_sessions_2017*`,
    UNNEST(hits) hits,
    UNNEST(product) product
  WHERE _table_suffix BETWEEN '0101' AND '0331'
  ),

  data_count AS(
  SELECT
    month,
    COUNTIF(action_type = '2') num_product_view,
    COUNTIF(action_type = '3') num_addtocart,
    COUNTIF(action_type = '6' AND productRevenue IS NOT NULL) num_purchase,
  FROM data_overview
  GROUP BY month
  ORDER BY month
  )

SELECT
  *,
  ROUND((num_addtocart/num_product_view * 100.0),2) add_to_cart_rate,
  ROUND((num_purchase/num_product_view * 100.0),2) purchase_rate
FROM data_count

Key Insights

  • Improving Funnel Efficiency Over Time: The Add-to-Cart Rate grew from 28.47% in January to 37.29% in March — a 31% relative improvement. This suggests either seasonal intent strengthening or product/UX changes beginning to take effect.
  • The Cart-to-Purchase Gap Remains the Biggest Leak: Despite healthy Add-to-Cart rates (~28–37%), the Purchase Rate sits at only 8–13%. This means roughly 65–72% of users who add to cart never complete a purchase — a cart abandonment problem.
  • March Outperforms: March shows the strongest conversion across all metrics — highest Add-to-Cart Rate (37.29%), highest Purchase Rate (12.64%), and highest absolute purchase volume (2,977). This could reflect a seasonal demand spike or a successful promotional push.

Recommendation

Actionable Strategy

Target Cart Abandonment (Priority #1): Since 65–72% of cart-adders never purchase, implement an automated cart abandonment email sequence triggered 1 hour, 24 hours, and 72 hours after cart inactivity. Include a time-limited offer (e.g., free shipping) to recover fence-sitters.

Replicate March's Success: Identify what drove the March spike — whether it was a promotion, SEO push, or UX change — and engineer those conditions deliberately in lower-performing months to lift the baseline Purchase Rate above 10%.

Case Study 2: Purchaser vs. Non-Purchaser Behavior

The Business Challenge

Marketing was spending budget equally on all visitors, but not all visitors are equal. The growth team needed to understand: what separates a buyer from a browser? Specifically, do purchasers engage more deeply with the site (as measured by pageviews per session), and does this pattern differ by month? Knowing this would justify whether personalizing depth-of-engagement signals is a viable path to higher conversion.

My Approach

1
Segment Definition: Defined Purchasers as users with totals.transactions >= 1 AND productRevenue IS NOT NULL. Non-Purchasers were users with transactions IS NULL AND productRevenue IS NULL — ensuring clean, mutually exclusive groups.
2
Metric Design: Computed Average Pageviews per Unique Visitor (SUM(pageviews) / COUNT(DISTINCT fullVisitorId)) for each segment to normalize for different user counts.
3
Safe Joining: Used a FULL JOIN (not INNER JOIN) between the two CTEs to prevent data loss in months where one segment might have zero representation.

Solution

SQL Query BigQuery (GA Sessions 2017) 
WITH
  t6_t7_data AS(
    SELECT
      FORMAT_DATE('%Y%m',PARSE_DATE('%Y%m%d',date)) month,
      totals.transactions,
      product.productRevenue,
      totals.pageviews,
      fullVisitorId
    FROM `bigquery-public-data.google_analytics_sample.ga_sessions_2017*`,
      UNNEST(hits) hits,
      UNNEST(product) product
    WHERE _table_suffix BETWEEN '0601' AND '0731'
  ),

  purchase AS(
    SELECT
      month,
      ROUND(SUM(pageviews) / COUNT(DISTINCT fullVisitorId), 8) avg_pageviews_purchase
    FROM t6_t7_data
    WHERE transactions >= 1 AND productRevenue IS NOT NULL
    GROUP BY month
  ),

  non_purchase AS(
    SELECT
      month,
      ROUND(SUM(pageviews) / COUNT(DISTINCT fullVisitorId), 8) avg_pageviews_non_purchase
    FROM t6_t7_data
    WHERE transactions IS NULL AND productRevenue IS NULL
    GROUP BY month
    ORDER BY month
  )

SELECT *
FROM purchase
FULL JOIN non_purchase
USING(month)
ORDER BY month

Query Result

Output — Avg Pageviews per User: Purchasers vs. Non-Purchasers (Jun–Jul 2017)
Month Avg Pageviews (Purchasers) Avg Pageviews (Non-Purchasers) Engagement Gap (×)
201706 94.02 316.87 3.4×
201707 124.24 334.06 2.7×

Key Insights

  • Non-Purchasers Browse 3× More Than Buyers: Counterintuitively, non-purchasers rack up 316–334 pageviews per user vs. only 94–124 for buyers. This is not a sign of high engagement — it's a signal of indecision and friction. Users are looping through pages without finding what converts them.
  • Purchasers Are Decisive: Buyers navigate with purpose. Their lower pageview counts (~94–124) suggest they found the right product quickly and completed the purchase without extensive browsing. This aligns with research showing that higher pageviews often correlate with confusion, not interest.
  • The Gap Narrowed in July: The engagement gap shrank from 3.4× in June to 2.7× in July, possibly because July's purchasers were more engaged (avg. 124 pageviews vs. 94), suggesting improved product discovery or stronger promotional targeting.

Recommendations

Actionable Strategy

Target High-Pageview Users with Exit-Intent Offers: Identify users who have exceeded ~200 pageviews in a session without adding to cart. Serve them a contextual pop-up (e.g., "Need help finding the right product?") or a personalized recommendation widget — intervening before they bounce.

Reduce Friction with Better Navigation: Since non-purchasers loop through many pages, invest in improving site search relevance, filtering UX, and product comparison features. The goal is to convert "wanderers" into "decisive shoppers" by surfacing the right product faster.
